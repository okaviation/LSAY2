Casting and auditions

I booked a studio at Theatre Deli following Karl's advice at the pitch to avoid online or home auditions. I created a form for auditionees to select their preferred time slots, then scheduled their auditions and sent them their designated times. 

We brought along the kit so the crew could familiarise themselves with it before the shoot day, and Karl joined us due to his casting experience. The auditions went well, with multiple candidates for each role, providing us with a good selection. Karl was invaluable, stepping in to act as the other character in duo scenes, which allowed us to see how the actors engaged with another person. We also ran multiple scenes with each auditionee and gave direction to see how they responded.

Table read and rehearsal

A few days after the auditions, Joanna and I reviewed all the performances, weighing up the pros and cons of each applicant. We decided to cast Jude Roch as Fred, Zainab Sullieman as Kaira, and Kyle Fair as Jonty. We then invited Jude and Zainab for a table read and rehearsal to ensure their chemistry was right before filming and to give them practice time together, making the shoot run more smoothly.

Both actors provided valuable input on the script, bringing up several points we later incorporated. Remember the Vanity Fair video in phase 2 with Denis Villeneuve where he said, "You can write something, but the actors can bring something even better than you were expecting"? That's exactly what happened here. 

We also conducted a timed run-through, which came out to 9 minutes and 20 seconds, quite good for a 9-page script!

Reflection on making so far

Casting.m4a

So why don't we see Jude in the final film?

With just 36 hours before the shoot, we received a message from Jude informing us that he no longer wanted to participate. He mentioned that some of the interactions didn't sit well with him. This was surprising, as he had been to my house and hugged me when he left, and our email exchanges had been conducted with the utmost professionalism. We had also made it very clear that this was a student project.

On one occasion, Jude had asked for the crew's numbers and social media handles, which we were reluctant to share due to many crew members being under 18 and GDPR concerns, I believe this may be a key turning point. Ultimately, we provided him with the names and gave him Karl's phone number, as Karl is an established director I have worked with. However, Jude did not contact Karl and decided to pull out of the project.

This led to a frantic scramble to find a replacement actor. The other candidates either had prior commitments or did not respond. We exhausted our contact list and connections, and posted on multiple casting sites. In the end, we had to offer a £75 flat fee to attract attention. We only managed to secure an actor at 01:00 on the shoot day.

Rescheduling

Due to Jude dropping out, I had to rearrange the filming schedule to accommodate the availability of the new actor. This proved challenging as he has a day job and lives in South Croydon, resulting in longer travel times. I revised the schedule approximately five times. Initially, we planned for a two-day shoot, but due to the actor's availability, we had to extend it another day. We also had to remove a scene from the script where he emerges from a Westminster office as we wouldn't have enough time to film it. Instead, we relocated the dialogue to be included over the credits.

Fortunately, our crew remained available, and the majority of locations were flexible in terms of scheduling. Unfortunately EPK had to be put on the backburner.

Reflection on the situation

Jude situation.m4a

Day 1

Day 1 involved shooting 4 scenes across 2 locations.

Location 1: the office

Our lead actor arrived late, which was fine as it gave us extra time to set up the set and, importantly, the craft table. The office space was excellent, featuring both natural light and an artificial skylight that illuminated the area nicely. Once the lead actor arrived, we got him changed into the suit provided by my Dad and applied the fake "S.A.R.S Menace" tattoo to his forearm.

A challenge we faced was the room heating up quickly. We couldn't have anyone wait outside due to it being a working building. To manage this, I opened the windows and turned on the air conditioning after each take. Unfortunately, the aircon began leaking halfway through, so we had to stop using it. I made sure everyone took adequate breaks outside for fresh air and stayed hydrated to prevent anyone from feeling faint. 

We finished the first two scenes ahead of schedule, allowing us to review the footage with the actors to ensure they were satisfied. I suggested doing a few extra takes of the film's intro scene, considering its significance. We wrapped up the other scene right on time at 19:45. My Mum then arrived with the Pret collection and drinks, providing a much-needed boost for the crew.

Location 2: the flat

During the car ride to the next location, I backed up the footage onto two drives. At the flat, we quickly set up the lights and began shooting. This process went relatively smoothly. As the night progressed, the crew became visibly tired, so I prioritised the shots to ensure we finished on time at 22:00.

Overall, I'm pleased with today's progress. We met the new actor for the first time, and Joanna seemed happy with the shots we captured, which is the most important thing.

Day 2

Day 2 involved shooting 3 scenes in 2 locations.

Location 1: Brick Lane

We all met at Brick Lane at 20:40. Arriving early, I took the opportunity to get some of the famous bagels. Although it threatened to rain, we were fortunate that it didn't. The location was exceptionally loud, situated near two tube lines and the lively Brick Lane. This wasn't the exact spot we had initially scouted; I had left the final decision to Joanna since she lives locally. Despite my frustration with the interruptions, our tight schedule left no room for relocation, so we pressed on, planning to use ADR in post-production if necessary. 

We were scheduled to leave by 22:00, but since we needed more time, I opted to wait a bit longer and booked a taxi to the next location instead of using public transport, reducing our travel time.

Location 2: Temple Tube

Upon arrival at Temple Tube, I had the DoP capture some establishing shots of the river reflections and skyline while we set up. This location, too, was noisier than expected due to heavy traffic. During our recce, it had been quiet with minimal traffic, but at 22:30 at night, there was a significant traffic noise. Again, if the audio was poor, ADR would be necessary. To mitigate the noise, I repeatedly pressed the traffic light button to stop the flow of vehicles, which occasionally worked. We proceeded with shooting using one artificial light source and a reflector, finishing about 20 minutes late. The crew was generally fine with this, although I let some members leave early due to their long journeys home.

Today could have gone better, and I partly take responsibility for not revisiting the locations at the actual times we would be filming. We'll assess the audio in post-production and hope ADR won't be necessary. Fortunately, I included a clause in the talent agreements allowing for re-recording audio if needed. 

The kit in the car.

Day 3

Day 3 involved shooting 2 scenes in 2 locations.

Today, without my Dad and his car, I had to cycle all the equipment up to the park, which was interesting! Once we arrived, the park was foggy, but the fog lifted by the time we started shooting. We found our unbolted bench and moved it to our desired location. Shooting went well, with only occasional interruptions from a distant lawnmower, which can be removed in post. We also had time to record shots not in Joanna’s shot list, like wide masters and different variations with the actors.

When it was time to leave, I tried to book a cab, but none were available. While trying all four of my taxi apps, I suggested the cast and crew visit the park zoo to boost their spirits. After 20 minutes of trying for a cab, I decided we should take the bus, as it would only take slightly longer than a cab at that time of day.

I cycled ahead to prepare the garage for our nightclub scene. Once they arrived, I set them up to begin blocking and remembered my Mum had a smoke machine and lights. I quickly cycled to her house to get them, which would significantly improve the scene’s look. When I returned, Joanna mentioned she wasn't feeling well, so I checked in with her, but she insisted we should finish the last scenes. The crew was visibly tired and agitated due to the early start, so my Mum came over with sandwiches and Starbucks to boost morale as we finished the final takes.

Overall, today went pretty well. I wish I had followed up on the lead I had for access to a club, as it would have looked much better, but due to the rescheduling, it’s unlikely we would have been able to use it on such short notice. Despite this, the footage looks good, and I can’t wait to see it in post. Now, it’s time to switch from making a film to watching one as I’m off to see Challengers with my DoP, Subomi.

Editing

Before touching the timeline, I meticulously organised all video and audio files, labelling them according to scene, shot and take. This systematic approach allowed me easy access to shots when editing and enabled Finder to automatically group related shots. I then categorised the files into separate "bins" in DaVinci Resolve, grouping them by each day's footage and audio.

Since the clips already contained a scratch track from the built-in camera microphone, synchronising footage and audio was a straightforward process, all I needed to do was find the clap. 

I then carefully reviewed all the takes for each scene, noting the best ones in my notepad. Additionally, I identified alternative takes that could serve as backups or contribute to a different emotional tone if needed.

Setting up the timeline was the next step. As I do not own DaVinci Resolve Studio, the timeline resolution was limited to 2K, so I configured it to 2K scope (2048 x 858). To ensure optimal viewing without black bars, I applied an adjustment clip over the entire timeline, zooming in by 1.26x to fill the screen entirely. 

I then compiled a list of credits so I would be ready when I got to the end of the film. 

Finally, I commenced the editing process. Below, you will find an initial rough cut of the edit followed by a more recent draft.

You may notice that I am uploading my edits using frame.io. This is because it is fast becoming the industry standard. Google Drive compresses uploads significantly, resulting in video artifacting. In contrast, frame.io maintains the original quality, keeping everything uncompressed and true to form.

I added the LSA leader and exported the project to the proper standards. Due to time constraints, I had to export without a colour grade. The audio and some pacing elements still need adjustment. I also realised I forgot to include the albert stamp!

The adjustment clip ensures that the footage fully fills the screen throughout the whole timeline, eliminating the need to individually adjust the zoom for each clip.
