Environmental Context

What is albert?

albert is a not-for-profit BAFTA owned organisation focused on promoting sustainability within the film and television industry. It provides resources, guidance, and certification programs to help productions reduce their environmental impact and operate in a more eco-friendly manner. albert offers tools such as carbon calculators, best practice guides, and training workshops to support industry professionals in adopting sustainable practices throughout all stages of production. By encouraging sustainable filmmaking practices, albert aims to mitigate the environmental footprint of the entertainment industry and promote a more environmentally responsible approach to filmmaking.

albert certification

In order to become albert grads, we took lessons where we learned about albert and their missions, as well as how to navigate and use the online portal. Following this, we completed a quiz to assess our understanding, and I achieved the top score!

albert case studies

We have utilised albert in various in-house productions, including the Lebedev's collaborative project and our TV show, the Sensational Showdown.

During the Lebedev's project, I was a member of the albert team. One of our primary strategies to minimise the carbon footprint for this project involved installing recycling points throughout the college. Additionally, we ensured the distribution of green memos and notes, which were also incorporated into call sheets to promote a culture of sustainability. Managing this on such a large-scale project was challenging, requiring multiple visits to different departments to determine the quantities of materials being used. The albert calculator's functionality was also making the process more difficult. Considering this experience, I will allocate additional time to ensure that albert is properly set up and documented for COBRA.

In the broader industry context, BBC One Drama "Blue Lights" achieved a 2-star Albert Certification relatively easily by employing local crews, disseminating green memos, and implementing department-specific sustainability goals. We plan to adopt similar strategies in COBRA as well. 

What will we do to keep our carbon emissions low?

We will promote the use of public transport whenever feasible, as transportation typically accounts for over 50% of a production's carbon emissions. In terms of catering, my Mum and I do a weekly collection from Pret, where we gather surplus food that is nearing its best-before date and distribute it to the local community through the Olio app. We can use this food to provide meals to our crew (with extras to take home as the collections are usually quite large) at no cost to the production whilst also saving food from landfill.

Additionally, I have sought out second-hand props to minimise the need for shipping and purchasing new items.

My intial ideas

Here are a few ideas for the EPK:

  • Semi-moving headshots: This concept, which I attempted to do in the collaboration project, involves capturing still headshots that transition into moving ones where characters perform actions typical of their roles, like smoking a cigar. These headshots could be used in promotional materials, such as bus stop advertisements.

  • Director’s walkthrough: Create a Vanity Fair-style director's walkthrough to give viewers a deeper understanding of the filmmaking process and creative choices.

  • Social media accounts: Set up social media profiles for the movie and its characters to engage the audience and build a following. We will need to do this anyway as there is a scene in the film where a character looks at a Twitter profile.

  • Additional cut for time scene: Film a short scene featuring the Brexit shrine, which was originally cut from the script. This scene will add a buzz factor to the film's marketing due to its over dramatic and absurd nature.

Reflection and preparation on past EPK

Having worked on an EPK during the collaborative project, I understand how challenging it can be to organise, especially when coordinating with the busy schedules of cast and crew. Typically, EPKs are handled by a separate person or team, but in our case, we have to manage it ourselves, which adds to the difficulty. To ensure everything runs smoothly, I need to prepare thoroughly and make prior plans for the EPK.

Case studies

In an interview discussing A24's marketing strategies, Loren Schwartz, known for his work in marketing at major studios including Warner Bros and Columbia Pictures, emphasised the importance of making more noise when working with limited resources, stating, "When you don’t have a lot of money, you need to make more noise somehow." 

As part of their marketing strategy for "Ex Machina," A24 created a Tinder account for one of the characters, Ava. This account engaged users in conversations, ultimately directing them to the film's Instagram page. Inspired by this approach, we can create a Twitter account for our main character Fred, allowing him to interact with the audience and generate buzz around our project. Given the inherently divisive nature of politics, his posts are likely to provoke strong reactions, leading people to quote tweet or reply.

We've seen a similar strategy semi-succeed on Twitter within the gaming industry. Riot Games introduced a Twitter account for an upcoming character in League of Legends named "Seraphine." The approach gained significant attention, amassing 320,000 followers over four months by tweeting as if the character were a real person, potentially misleading some users who were unaware of the backstory. However, the account never explicitly acknowledged its affiliation with LoL and started to stir controversy when it began posting that it was experiencing depressive episodes, anxiety, and imposter syndrome. Naturally, this upset the community, as it was seen as mocking and overly parasocial, given that the account was not a real person. Shortly after this controversy, the account stopped tweeting. It's essential to note that such campaigns can quickly turn sour, so I'll ensure to carefully vet the tweets and discuss their potential impacts with Joanna.

Creating a professional email and website

We wanted to establish a production company with a professional email domain rather than the typical "@gmail.com". Over a few days, we brainstormed ideas using random word generators and our own creativity. Despite having several ideas, many were already taken or lacked available domains. Eventually, we settled on "markit pictures", drawing inspiration from the film set terminology. I purchased the domain through Cloudflare and created a free website on Adobe Portfolio, which is included in my Creative Cloud plan, to enhance our professional image with a simple landing page.

I then chose to set up the email (hello@markit.pictures) through iCloud to avoid monthly fees. However, this proved to be a complex process due to changes Google had implemented which makes it harder for someone to set up a custom email for free. This required a lot of troubleshooting and creative solutions to make sure it worked properly but I got it working in the end!

Legal

One of my jobs was dealing with legal documents such as risk assessments, talent release forms and crew forms.

Talent release and crew forms:

I decided to create my own forms because I found the LSA template too simple and wanted to include my own additional clauses. I drew from forms used in productions I’ve worked on and I also researched online to ensure I wasn't missing anything important. I decided to include a clause allowing cast and crew to use parts of our film in their showreels, which I consider essential since they are participating for free and is a detail often missing from standard contracts.

To distribute these forms, I used a free service called DocuSeal. This platform guides users through the form clearly and allows me to track their status, such as whether they have been opened or completed, which means I can easily and effectively follow up if someone has not filled their agreement out.

Risk assessment and public liability

Fortunately, I have the advantage of having a father who works in the safety industry. He shared with me the risk assessment he typically uses, which I then adjusted by simplifying and trimming down certain elements and tailoring the risks to those of a film set.

DIT and dailies

What is a DIT?

The Digital Imaging Technician configures camera menus properly, manages media, and ingests footage. On large film sets with a video village, they ensure that LUTs are applied correctly, so producers and other stakeholders aren’t just looking at dull log footage. 

I have previously worked as a DIT on several shoots, primarily handling media ingestion. I enjoy the role and the technical aspects of working with media and computers.

On COBRA

On this project, I will serve as the DIT, responsible for media ingestion and creating dailies when requested. The raw files were quite large, consuming about 3GB per minute. I used Adobe Media Encoder to create dailies, experimenting with different settings to find the best balance between quality and file size. I settled on MP4 at 1080p with an inbuilt Rec709 LUT. Normally, dailies would be sent out with a custom show LUT, developed by the director, DoP, and others, but I didn’t have time to learn how to create one. These dailies are much smaller in size and easily shareable with the crew members who need to see them. 

I backed up the footage onto two different types of drives (SSD and HDD) and verified that the footage had transferred correctly and played back properly. The drives were then stored separately, with one in my possession and the other at my Dad’s place.

Location scouting

As the location manager and 1st AD, one of my responsibilities was to arrange routes and schedule our scouting trips. We conducted two location scouts, capturing test shots on my iPhone along the way. 

1st location scout

We began at Old Street, where Joanna introduced me to two promising locations in Shoreditch. From there, we travelled to Temple to visit a picturesque spot with views of the London Eye and the Houses of Parliament. Our next stop was Westminster to scout streets for the final scene, but our plans were interrupted by ongoing protests. Instead of heading to Battersea Park as planned, we received a call about a potential office location in Staples Corner, which we prioritised and visited immediately. Afterward, we scouted a house and a flat for scene 3. 

2nd location scout

Our second scout started in Park Royal, where we had a look at a warehouse for the club scene. We then visited two potential parks for the film's climax. At the second park, we found benches that had been moved by a recent storm. This was great since it meant the benches weren't bolted down, allowing us to reposition them on the shoot day to capture the perfect shot.

Call sheets

As the 1st AD, my job included creating call sheets. This task was straightforward for me as I had practised it in class and made them on previous projects. I utilised what3words, an emerging service that divides the world into 3-metre squares, each with a unique combination of words. This is particularly useful in locations like parks where there are no specific landmarks and coordinates can be bulky.

I also added the relevant parts of the script to the end of the call sheet for easy access, ensuring that actors or crew members had an easy reference in case they misplaced their scripts.

Diversity and inclusion breakdown

Final Draft has a handy feature that provides inclusivity reports if you properly label the characters. This helps us ensure an equal representation of genders and allows us to see what types of roles they are playing. This feature is particularly useful in the industry, as shows often need to fill out diversity statements, such as a Diamond statement, to give organisations a clear understanding of who is appearing on screen.

We decided against sending out individual questionnaires that delve deeply into diversity, as we felt it would be too intrusive for a project in which participants were involved for free. 

For COBRA, we can see there is a fairly even dialogue split between male and female roles so no immediate action is needed.

Props and costume

I sourced the majority of props and costumes needed for the film.

For props, I specifically wanted a green banker’s lamp, which I felt was synonymous with MPs' offices. I asked friends and family, but no one had one. Then, I tried Trash Nothing, a service where people give away items they no longer want, but had no luck. Finally, I found a second-hand one on eBay for £19.

We also needed a UKIP rosette for the initial scene where Fred is recording a UKIP message. I searched many websites as we needed fast shipping. One site was cheap and fast, but they required payment by cheque! In the end, I had no choice but to buy it from the official UKIP shop for £4, including shipping. I was quite worried as it hadn't turned up, but the rosette finally arrived on the morning of filming. As a bonus, it came with UKIP leaflets, which we used for decoration in the office scenes.

For costumes, the male lead needed a suit. I asked my Dad to send over a selection of suits, and then I sent them to Joanna for her to choose from. Fortunately, they fit, even though the actor was taller and had a different build than my dad. We also provided trousers and shoes for him.

Additionally, we needed a folder and printed photos for the case file. I am fortunate to have a small photo printer at home, so I used it to print the photos. I then went to WHSmith to find the folder Joanna wanted. Unfortunately, they didn't have the exact one, but I found a similar one.

Scheduling

As the 1st AD, my job was to schedule the film. I created a Google Form to send out to the crew and cast to gather information about any allergies, medical and accessibility requirements, preferences for the craft table, their transportation methods for albert purposes, and, most importantly, their availability for filming.

Once I received all the responses, I compiled them into a Google Sheet to identify the most suitable times for everyone. I began scheduling by first inputting locations that were only available at specific times, followed by the availability of key crew members (excluding non-essential roles like the BTS photographer). I also considered the sun's position to maintain continuity.

A significant change was required for scene 8, originally set at sunrise but planned to be filmed backwards at sunset. This proved too logistically challenging, so we decided, as previously discussed during the scriptwriting process, to set this scene on a new day, which also made more sense for the story.

Scheduling was further complicated because Joanna had split continuous shots into separate shot numbers in her shot list, based on how she envisioned the final piece. For example, shots 2 to 5 were actually just two continuous shots. I asked her to merge them into their actual shots, which helped me better visualise and allocate time effectively.

Accounting

I was also responsible for accounting and tracking expenses. Early on, we decided against crowdfunding as we felt we lacked the time to raise a substantial amount. We also felt that if we accepted people's money, we would prefer to have more time to produce the film and not have a deadline looming over our heads. Consequently, we are funding the film out of our own pockets. 

We are trying to minimise costs wherever possible, covering only travel within London zones 1-6 and sourcing as much free food as possible. Any additional expenses will be paid on the spot, as there is no set budget since it's all self-funded. I didn't use any specialised software for this, just kept track in my notes app.
