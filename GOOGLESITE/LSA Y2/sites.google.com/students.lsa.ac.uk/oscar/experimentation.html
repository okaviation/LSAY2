Choosing kit

After pitching to Karl and Kevin, they generously offered us the use of their equipment. They have a variety of options available, and I am considering either the FX9 or the S1H, both of which are Netflix certified. The S1H is more versatile but the FX9 is more familiar as we have used it in college. I consulted a friend in the technical pathway for their opinion on which to choose. Our conversation is on the right.

Regarding artistic shots with different cameras, it's worth noting that up to 10% of a program's final total runtime can be shot on a non-Netflix approved camera which means that we can capture B-Roll easily once we take the camera back.

I watched several YouTube videos about films shot on inexpensive cameras, and the main takeaway was that the right camera for me doesn't have to be very expensive. What truly matters is the vision and the set.

At this point, you might be wondering why I am using Netflix specifications. The reason is that these documents are publicly available and serve as a widely accepted benchmark in the industry. This approach also aligns with AO1, as I am applying specialist industry practices.

After testing the S1H, I decided to choose it due to its compact size, versatility, and excellent high-resolution 4K capabilities. I opted to shoot in full-frame 4K (17:9) at 4:2:2/10-bit colour and 24fps, following Netflix and industry specifications. This setup will allow us to properly colour grade the footage and crop up to 6% while maintaining UHD quality, ensuring professional-grade results and flexibility in post-production.

Testing the camera and edit software

Recording in 10-bit V-Log means we have to colour grade the footage due to its initial "flat" appearance. 

Since Premiere Pro couldn't process the .MOV files, I turned to DaVinci Resolve and tested its built-in LUT, which transforms colours to Rec709. Rec709 is a standard colour space for devices, ensuring consistent colour reproduction across different devices. While Rec709 is used for quick viewing and dailies, we still retain the flexibility to choose our final colour grading in post-production. 

Prior to this project, I hadn't worked with DaVinci Resolve, but I found it to be significantly more intuitive compared to Premiere Pro. I found its node-based system for colour correction much more logical to work with.

Practitioner / EPK research

I find a lot of inspiration in Denis Villeneuve's work, especially his recent Dune films, which have kept me motivated throughout FMP. 

Here are the three key points I took from this Vanity Fair video:

  1. Don’t be afraid to go back to somewhere you filmed to get authentic sounds.

  2. You can write something but the actors can bring something even better than you were expecting.

  3. In Part Two, he didn’t use any artificial light in the desert which brought high realism and strong tactile emotion.

I intend to incorporate the absence of artificial lighting into COBRA, and I'll certainly remember the other points. Additionally, I'm keen on creating a director's breakdown similar to what was shown in the video for my EPK.

In the behind-the-scenes footage of Dune: Part One, Denis emphasises the importance of building authentic sets to influence the actors' performances and spark their imagination. While our film may not be a sci-fi, I aim to apply this principle to our project's locations. Rather than relying on green screens or constructing artificial sets, we'll immerse our actors by filming in real working environments, such as an actual office setting, enhancing their engagement and the authenticity of their performances.

Contextual research

Cultural Integration

Cultural integration plays a crucial role in the film, as our main character, Fred, makes significant efforts to conform to British values in order to blend in. We've further emphasised this by depicting him as a member of the UKIP party, known for its right-wing stance and opposition to immigrants like Fred and his family.

Some history:

The UK has a significantly longer history of large-scale immigration compared to many other European nations. In 1966, Home Secretary Roy Jenkins shared a vision of integration that did not involve the loss of immigrants' national characteristics and culture. 

He stated, "I do not regard [integration] as meaning the loss, by immigrants, of their own national characteristics and culture. I do not think that we need in this country a ‘melting pot’, which will turn everybody out in a common mould, as one of a series of carbon copies of someone’s misplaced vision of the stereotyped Englishman… I define integration, therefore, not as a flattening process of assimilation but as equal opportunity, accompanied by cultural diversity, in an atmosphere of mutual tolerance." 

This perspective led to early legislation against discrimination, such as the 1965 Race Relations Act, which was ahead of its time by European standards. The UK adopted a generally sympathetic attitude towards allowing cultural and religious exemptions to laws and practices. For instance, Sikh motorcyclists were permitted to wear turbans instead of helmets, and Muslim police women were allowed to wear the hijab on duty. The underlying belief was that this would help minorities feel part of the wider community, creating a sense of unity. However, this ideal was often far from reality. The early 1980s saw riots in many British cities against racism, particularly in the police who faced widespread criticism for institutional racism. 

In more recent times, specifically under the Cameron-Clegg coalition, legislation was introduced to teach school children about "British Values." This initiative aimed to promote tolerance, mutual respect, and understanding of the diverse cultural landscape within the UK. By embedding these values in the education system, the government hoped to foster a more cohesive and inclusive society, preparing young people to live harmoniously in a multicultural nation.

In contrast to the UK, Norwegian assimilation policies have taken a noticeably different approach. In Norway, the focus has traditionally been on promoting a "homogeneous national identity", expecting immigrants to adopt Norwegian language and cultural norms more fully. This approach encourages immigrants to blend into the mainstream culture rather than maintaining distinct cultural identities. This policy has sparked debate and controversy, as it often places pressure on immigrants to abandon their heritage in favour of Norwegian customs (such as what happened to the Sami people), leading to challenges in preserving cultural diversity within the country. 

I mention Norway because a similar assimilation process is depicted in the early episodes of the 2012 series Lilyhammer. Here, the main character, an American man settling in Norway, navigates through sessions at NAV (Norwegian Labour and Welfare Administration) where he learns about Norwegian history and culture. Initially perceiving it as bureaucratic and tedious, he later pressures another student who is not participating, indicating his partial conformity to the process. However, at the end of the day he remains distinctly American, and the assimilation process only reinforces his American roots.

Despite the points discussed, we still witness many immigrants in the UK striving to adopt and conform to British values in an effort to blend in. COBRA will aim to demonstrate the outcomes of what happens when you completely abandon your heritage to fit in. 

Brexit

Our film unfolds against the backdrop of a recent "Brexit victory."
Brexit, where 51.89% of voters voted to leave the EU, continues to have significant repercussions on the UK. This makes it an opportune time to create a film surrounding it. Brexit continues to dominate discussions in British news, with frequent coverage. In Google trend rankings, the term "Brexit" remains at 3%, showcasing sustained interest, especially when compared to other global issues such as Ukraine, which hovers around 7% despite an active conflict.

Collecting audience data

In order to collect audience data, I created a Google Form and distributed it among my friends and family.

Key aspects I aimed to explore included: 

  • Age - although primarily targeting younger individuals, I also gathered responses from older demographics. 

  • The influence of ethnicity on political perspectives.

  • Opinions regarding Brexit and levels of trust in the government.

  • Preferences regarding the type of content audiences would be willing to watch. 

Here are the findings from the survey:

  • I received responses from a wide age range, I was not just limited to younger demographics.

  • Across all age groups, there's a lack of substantial trust in the current government (Sunak Conservative).

  • Opinions on Brexit and the government have remained largely consistent since the referendum.

  • There's a fairly even distribution between those who would and wouldn't watch a political short film set against the backdrop of Brexit.

  • Respondents indicated a preference for political films with a comedic element.

  • It's noteworthy that older white men showed greater interest in UK politics, although the dataset for this demographic is relatively small.

Job role case study: 1st AD

What is a 1st AD?

A first assistant director (1st AD) is the director's right-hand person. The 1st AD plans the filming schedule and collaborates with all heads of departments to ensure a smooth shoot. They also lead location recces and input the script into industry-standard software for breakdowns. During filming, the 1st AD manages the set, allowing the director to focus on creating the best shots without distraction.

I have had the opportunity to observe and work closely with established ADs like Martin Serene, providing me with valuable insights into the role. I’ve also taken on parts of the AD role while working as a producer, handling tasks such as creating schedules and keeping the crew on track.

I then watched a video featuring Joshua Lou Friedman, a seasoned 1st AD. He shared insights into the role:

- "It's my job to adapt to the director and make sure he gets what he wants. Sometimes, I ensure he gets what he needs rather than what he wants, especially if we're running behind."

- The job is demanding, with little time off.

- "The schedule is your baby."

- It's a balancing act between being jovial and stern.
